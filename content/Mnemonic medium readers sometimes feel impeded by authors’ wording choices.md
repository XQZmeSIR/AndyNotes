# Mnemonic medium readers sometimes feel impeded by authors’ wording choices

In the feedback to [[How to write good prompts]], a number of readers report feeling that they’ve had difficulty answering some prompts because they’re framed in terms of my wording. Not that my wording is bad or wrong—it’s just that these readers feel that if _they_ had worded the question themselves, they’d have chosen different phrasing which would have stuck better for them, personally.

We didn’t get this feedback in reports on Quantum Country; I think it’s an issue specific to this being a _non-technical_ text. The wording complaint arises because there isn’t a fixed, pre-specified way to refer to some notion, which makes it impossible to follow the [[Expert response heuristic, after Issa Rice]]. The author had to come up with terms themselves, and the reader feels they might have used different ones. This happens in technical texts, too, but less frequently: the referents are more often precisely specifiable.

This complaint was mostly focused on cloze deletion prompts, which are extra- dependent on issues of wording: [[Cloze deletions often create frustrations in non-technical mnemonic texts]]

More broadly speaking, [[The mnemonic medium should give readers control over the prompts they collect]].
